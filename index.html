Drift

Self-Driving Retreat · 2026

Type

Self-initiated

Year

2026

Tools

Rhino · Blender

Role

Solo

The ProblemWhen a vehicle drives itself, the dashboard disappears, and the interior is free to stop being a “car.” So what should it become?

Drift is a self-driving RV interior for one or two travelers. It's a compact, lived-in retreat built to let technology fade into the background, encouraging integration with nature.

Self-driving technology allows the vehicle to drive itself, opening up travel to those who can't or no longer drive. This is perfect for solo wanderers and elderly couples who still want the freedom of the open road without being behind the wheel. It's a direct nod to autonomy and independence.

Once the vehicle drives itself, the cabin is freed from the dashboard. The bed sits against a wide picture window, opening the space to the landscape. The interior uses a wabi-sabi material palette of warm wood, paper, tatami, and soft textiles, keeping the space feeling honest and lived-in rather than showroom-luxurious.

The interior draws inspiration from Japandi spaces for their feelings of quiet, grounded calm.

The large windows are inspired by Japanese trains; while driving past scenic routes, travelers can drink tea or play board games and gaze out at the passing scene.

Mood & CMF

A color, material & finish study. Lots of warm woods, paper and tatami greens, soft indigo textiles and gingham, with brushed gold and nostalgic pops. The palette anchors the wabi-sabi, lived-in feeling the whole interior is built around. The vibe is playful, nostalgic to childhood summers and late night video games.

Process

Concept & Planning

A getaway for two

Early notes of my intent: a Japandi-style retreat for elders or couples, where self-driving makes long-distance travel safe and independent. No dashboard, no "car features". Instead, a tea space for scenic, train-like rides. The small layout encourages connection with yourself and your companion.

Layout Explorations

Scrapped: too "car-like"

The first layout I tried relied on car logic: rotatable seats, a slide-along TV, a deployable ramp. They worked, but kept the space feeling like a vehicle. I scrapped them to make proper use of the freed space —if the car drives itself, it shouldn't need "car features" at all. 

Final Concept

A room, not a vehicle

The resolved plan reads like a small home: kitchen and bath at one end, a living/tea space in the middle, and a quiet bed nook separated at the far end. Large shoji-screen-like windows (inspired by Japanese bullet trains) wrap around the bed nook, making the landscape the view from bed. Shallow accessible stairs are deployed for the elderly, which can also be replaced with a ramp.

Modeling in Rhino

Building it out

Modeling the interior in Rhino, in chronological order. After many iterations, I later moved the bed to the very end of the cabin. It was very important to me to find the best "relaxation nook," wrapped on three sides by glass, perfect for bird watching.

FINAL RENDERS IN BLENDER!

Reflection

Drift became a room rather than a vehicle — a quiet, lived-in retreat that opens to the landscape instead of the road. If I took it further, I’d pressure-test the layout at full scale and detail exactly how the bed, stairs, and storage fold and move.

Iris

Work in Progress

Smart Eyewear · 2026

A current project, still in progress. I'm prototyping the electronics and the frame — renders and build photos to come!

Self-initiated

Year

2026

Tools

Arduino · Rhino · Figma

Role

Solo

How long can the eye stare at a screen before it strains? Iris is eyewear that gently reminds you to rest. No stressful numbers, just a calm cue.

Inspired by my love for Gentle Monster and cyberpunk aesthetics, Iris is a fashion line of smart eyewear that targets eye strain from digital devices. 

Concept

A fashion piece first

To target consumers, the frames have are not only fashionable, but also a support to your health. 

It's calm tech: Iris lets you know when it's time to take a break through a soft, calming stimulus like a vibrational pulse at the temple.

Early Sketches

First passes, then pared back

Early prototype sketches: clear and silver frames with the electronics and internal hardware drawn in, plus diamond-stud accents and a notched, rounded temple in cross-section. I later moved away from this heavier build toward something more compact and minimal, for a better, more comfortable everyday wear.

Material Research

Clear, lasting, a little cyberpunk

  • A clear frame in a material that won't yellow over time — leaning toward a clear gray rather than fully clear.
  • Silver and metal detailing for the cyberpunk vibe.
  • Lost-wax casting for the metal parts.
  • Blue-light lenses to ease screen strain.

How It Works

The 20-minute loop

Switch on. A touch of the button starts a 20-minute timer as you settle in front of a screen, head mostly still.

The nudge. At 20 minutes, the glasses pulse — a soft cue that it's time to rest your eyes.

30-second cool-down. One pulse to begin, two to finish. The motion sensor checks that your head is moving and encourages a few extra blinks.

Reset. The glasses return to their normal state, and the whole cycle loops.

The App

A progress bar, not a number

A 6-axis IMU (gyroscope + accelerometer) inside the frames reads the head's movement and sends it to the phone over Bluetooth Low Energy. The app doesn't care where you're looking, only that the coordinates are changing.

When you're still and staring, a progress bar fills. When you look around, it pauses and drains. At full, the app tints the lenses for a 30-second refresh, then clears them and resets — no stressful numbers, just a calm bar.

Modeling Prototype 01

The first build

My first Rhino build: a rimless oval frame with slim metal temples and a clear hardware housing. It looked refined, but the temples carried a lot of weight and hardware. I wanted the product to feel more compact, and a bit more stylized. 

Modeling Prototype 02

Building it in Rhino

Modeling the second prototype in Rhino: working out how the temple hardware, hinge, and the lens module pack into a slimmer frame. Red points mark diamond inlays for a luxury touch.
